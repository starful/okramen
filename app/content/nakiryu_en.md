## The Soul of the Shop: History and Philosophy.

In the quiet, unassuming residential neighborhood of Otsuka in Toshima, Tokyo, stands a temple dedicated to the art of the noodle. Nakiryu, which translates to "Crying Dragon," is not merely a restaurant; it is a testament to the obsessive pursuit of culinary equilibrium. Founded in 2012 by Chef Kazumasa Saito, Nakiryu rose to global prominence when it became the second ramen shop in history to be awarded a prestigious Michelin star. While many Michelin-recognized establishments lean into opulence, Nakiryu maintains a humble, focused integrity that mirrors the personality of its creator.

Chef Saito’s background is rooted in the rigorous discipline of Chinese cuisine. Before opening Nakiryu, he honed his skills at the renowned Mistress of Chinese Cuisine and later served as the head chef at the legendary Hong Kong ramen shop, MIST. It was this cross-cultural expertise that allowed him to bridge the gap between traditional Chinese Tantanmen (Dan Dan Mian) and the refined sensibilities of Japanese Ramen. Saito-san’s philosophy is built on the concept of "balance over bombast." He does not seek to overwhelm the palate with a single note of heat or fat; instead, he aims to create a "Dragon’s Cry"—a resonant, lingering flavor that echoes long after the meal is finished.

The shop itself, with its modest ten-seat counter, operates with the precision of a laboratory and the heart of a home. There is no bravado here, only the focused clatter of bowls and the rhythmic straining of noodles. For Saito-san, the Michelin star is not a trophy to be displayed but a standard to be upheld daily. This commitment to consistency is why pilgrims from across the globe descend upon Toshima, standing in line for hours for a chance to taste what is widely considered the best ramen in Tokyo. Nakiryu represents the peak of the "Gourmet Ramen" movement, proving that a humble bowl of soup can possess the complexity and soul of a multi-course tasting menu.

## The Broth Analysis: Deep dive into ingredients and complexity.

To understand why Nakiryu’s broth is heralded as a masterpiece, one must look beyond the surface of its creamy, sunset-hued Tantanmen. The broth is a multi-dimensional liquid tapestry, woven from dozens of ingredients that are layered with scientific precision. While most ramen shops rely on a single base, Nakiryu utilizes a sophisticated "triple-stock" method that provides a foundation of unparalleled depth.

The primary base is a poultry-forward infusion, utilizing whole chickens and chicken carcasses (torigara) simmered for hours at a controlled temperature to extract collagen and clean protein notes without the muddiness of a rolling boil. To this, Saito-san adds beef bones, which introduce a subtle, rounded richness that chicken alone cannot achieve. The third layer is a delicate dashi made from high-quality seafood—typically katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes), niboshi (dried sardines), and kombu (kelp). This seafood element doesn’t make the broth "fishy"; rather, it provides a "marine umami" that acts as a bridge between the heavy meat proteins and the sharp seasonings.

For the signature Tantanmen, the broth undergoes a further transformation. The base stock is emulsified with a house-made sesame paste (zhishejiang). Unlike commercial pastes, Nakiryu’s version is made from sesame seeds that are roasted in-house to a specific degree of darkness to ensure a nutty, toasted aroma that isn't bitter. This creates a velvety, cream-like texture that coats the tongue, providing a cooling counterpoint to the heat.

Then comes the "Fire and Acid"—the most critical part of the Nakiryu experience. The heat is derived from a complex house-made chili oil (ra-yu) infused with cinnamon, star anise, and Sichuan peppercorns. This provides a "Ma-La" sensation—a tingling numbness coupled with a warm glow—rather than a sharp, painful burn. To cut through the richness of the sesame and the fat of the poultry, Saito-san introduces a sharp, sophisticated acidity through a blend of vinegars, including premium black vinegar. 

The resulting broth is a marvel of engineering. When you first sip it, you are greeted by the nutty sweetness of the sesame. A second later, the savory depth of the chicken and beef takes over. As you swallow, the gentle heat of the chili oil begins to tingle, only to be immediately refreshed by a clean, vinegary brightness that resets your palate for the next bite. This cycle of flavor prevents the "palate fatigue" often associated with rich ramen, making every spoonful as exciting as the first. It is a masterclass in culinary counterpoints, achieving a level of complexity that justifies its status as the best ramen in Tokyo, Toshima. This broth is not just soup; it is a liquid narrative of Chef Saito’s entire career.

## Noodle & Topping Harmony: Texture, Chashu, and Ajitama analysis.

In the world of elite ramen, the noodles are often referred to as the "protagonist," and at Nakiryu, they are treated with the reverence of a fine silk. Unlike many shops that outsource their noodles to specialized factories, Nakiryu produces its own noodles daily on the premises. For the Tantanmen, Chef Saito has engineered a specific type of "Hoso-men" (thin, straight noodle) that possesses a distinctively firm, "al dente" bite.

The flour used is a proprietary blend of Japanese wheat, selected for its high protein content and aromatic quality. These noodles are crafted to be slightly porous, allowing them to cling to the creamy sesame broth, ensuring that every strand carries the perfect ratio of liquid and solid. The texture is key; there is a satisfying "snap" when you bite into them, providing a structural contrast to the liquid-heavy bowl.

The toppings at Nakiryu are equally intentional, designed to enhance rather than distract. In the Tantanmen, the traditional "soboro" (minced pork) is a standout. Instead of being a mere garnish, the pork is seasoned with a sweet and savory soy-based sauce and stir-fried until it develops a concentrated umami profile. As you eat, these small morsels of meat settle into the bottom of the bowl, creating "treasure hunts" of flavor in the later stages of the meal.

For those who opt for the Shoyu Ramen or add extra toppings, the Chashu is a revelation. Nakiryu offers two styles: a classic braised pork belly and a low-temperature sous-vide pork loin. The braised version is tender and melt-in-your-mouth, while the sous-vide version is floral, pink, and incredibly succulent, reminiscent of high-end roast beef. 

The Ajitama (marinated soft-boiled egg) is the crowning jewel. The white is firm but delicate, having been marinated in a soy-dashi blend for hours. When punctured, the yolk flows out—not watery, but with the consistency of warm honey. The richness of the yolk adds another layer of creaminess to the broth, creating a luxurious mouthfeel that elevates the entire experience. Every element, from the crunch of the fresh green onions to the subtle earthiness of the bamboo shoots (menma), is calibrated to support the "Crying Dragon" theme.

## The Experience: Vibe, wait time, and neighborhood guide.

Visiting Nakiryu is a lesson in patience and reward. Located just a short walk from Otsuka Station on the JR Yamanote Line, the shop sits in a part of Toshima that feels worlds away from the neon chaos of Shinjuku or Shibuya. Otsuka is one of the few remaining areas in Tokyo where you can still feel the "Shitamachi" (old town) vibe, with its vintage streetcars (the Toden Arakawa Line) and narrow alleys.

The wait at Nakiryu is legendary and serves as a rite of passage for any true ramen aficionado. Because the shop only seats ten and holds a Michelin star, the line can easily stretch to two or three hours. However, the shop has recently implemented a more modern queuing system involving a numbered ticketing process at certain times to mitigate the physical strain on diners. It is highly recommended to arrive at least 30–45 minutes before opening or to check their official social media for the latest entry rules.

Once you step inside, the atmosphere is one of "focused serenity." The interior is minimalist—clean wood, muted tones, and soft lighting. It is remarkably quiet for a ramen shop; there is no loud shouting or frantic music. Instead, there is a reverent hush, occasionally broken by the steam of the noodle boiler and the sound of guests slurping their noodles in appreciative silence. This is "Solo Friendly" dining at its finest; the individual counter seats allow you to commune directly with your bowl without distraction.

After your meal, take the time to explore Toshima. You are just one stop away from Sugamo, known as the "Harajuku for Grandmas," which offers a fascinating look at traditional Japanese sweets and textiles. Alternatively, you can head toward Ikebukuro for high-end shopping and entertainment. But for most who visit Nakiryu, the ramen is the destination itself. You leave the shop feeling a sense of culinary enlightenment, having tasted what happens when technical mastery meets a humble heart. It is, without question, the best ramen in Tokyo, Toshima, and a defining experience of the modern Japanese gastronomic landscape.
